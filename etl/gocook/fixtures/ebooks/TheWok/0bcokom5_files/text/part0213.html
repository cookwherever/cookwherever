The Wok: Recipes and Techniques

Why don’t people deep-fry at home more often? The most common answers I hear are: it’s messy, it’s expensive (“What do I do with all the leftover oil?”), it’s dangerous, and it’s unhealthy.

Well, a wok can certainly help solve your first three problems, and we’ll discuss how in detail. You’re on your own for the fourth. Frying adds fat to your food, period. Contrary to the popular myth that frying at a high enough temperature reduces the amount of oil food absorbs, the opposite is in fact the case: the crisper and less soggy your fried food tastes, the more oil it has likely absorbed during cooking (and we’ll get to the explanation shortly). If you’re worried about the health aspects, eat a smaller portion of your General Tso’s chicken or invite five friends over before you make a dozen fried spring rolls so that you’re limited to two. Even better, get a gym membership or add a dog or a child to the family and burn those calories chasing them down to make sure that things not meant to be peed on are not peed on.

In this chapter we’ll cover panfrying, dry frying, and deep frying in their various incarnations, including breaded, dredged, and battered foods and foods that you fry naked (that is, the food is naked, not you, unless you feel like living especially dangerously).
